# Workpiece Fit

Use this reference when a user asks whether a rotary cup powder coating head is suitable for a specific workpiece. Treat these as field-oriented patterns from customer conversations, not absolute guarantees.

## Strong Fit

### Electrical cabinets, control cabinets, box corners, and inner angles

Good fit when:

- The problem is poor powder coverage in cabinet corners, inner angles, or seams.
- Operators currently spray the main surface online, then do liquid touch-up or manual rework offline.
- The line speed makes it hard for a conventional gun to slowly chase every corner.

Why rotary cup helps:

- Conventional gun airflow can drive powder along seams and away from corners.
- Rotary cup powder clouds are softer and more dispersed, which can reduce powder being blown out of the corner.

Ask:

- Cabinet size, corner depth, line speed, current gun brand, and whether touch-up is online or offline.

### Rolled edges, folded edges, grooves, and irregular small parts

Good fit when:

- The part has rolled edges or a groove where too much powder creates buildup, but too little powder leaves bare metal.
- The part is a small stamping, die casting, grid, or small recessed part.
- Operators need to constantly change angle or slow down to cover recesses.

Why rotary cup helps:

- It can reduce dependence on operator skill.
- It can make recess coverage faster on suitable small parts.

Suggested answer:

"This is a typical rotary cup use case, especially if the part is repeatable and the groove is open enough for the powder cloud to enter. Control powder output and avoid heavy buildup."

### Heat sinks, light housings, energy storage ribs, and inverter housings

Good fit with caution when:

- The part has fins, ribs, narrow slots, or deep heat-dissipation grooves.
- The current issue is outside buildup or edge burn while the inner groove remains light.

Important boundaries:

- A slot around 10 mm wide and up to about 80 mm deep is a practical reference range for discussion, but still requires workpiece photos or test spraying.
- Heat sink structures around 100 mm deep and 10 mm wide are difficult. Rotary cup may improve quality, but speed and one-pass results should be discussed conservatively.
- If the nozzle cannot physically reach or aim into the useful area, do not promise a solution.

Ask:

- Groove width, groove depth, opening direction, target film thickness, powder type, and whether the part is hung or laid flat.

### Fence posts, guardrails, aluminum art doors, shutter doors, and louver parts

Good fit when:

- The part has many corners, slanted areas, holes, tubes, or repeated shapes.
- The customer wants better film consistency and less dependence on highly skilled manual spraying.

Important boundaries:

- Experienced operators may already spray these parts well with conventional guns.
- Rotary cup can improve ease and consistency, but the customer should compare against their current process.

## Possible Fit, Needs More Data

### Large panels, container doors, thick film, sand texture, metallic powder, and wrinkle powder

Use caution when:

- The issue is not recess coverage but thick film, texture stability, orange peel, pinholes, or heavy decorative powder.
- The customer wants fast powder buildup on a large surface.

Guidance:

- Ask about target film thickness, powder type, curing profile, current voltage/current, line speed, gun distance, and surface texture requirement.
- Do not recommend rotary cup as the first answer before checking powder, curing, and electrostatic settings.

### Automatic lines, reciprocators, and robot stations

Possible fit when:

- Product types are repeatable.
- The customer has multiple guns and wants better consistency.
- The rotary cup can be mounted without interfering with motion, cable routing, airflow, or clearance.

Ask:

- Manual, reciprocator, automatic gun, or robot?
- Number of guns.
- Gun spacing and available mounting space.
- Whether the customer wants one test unit before batch retrofit.

## Poor Fit Or High Risk

### Deep closed cavities or narrow slots where the nozzle cannot enter

Do not overpromise when:

- The opening is too narrow.
- The cavity is too deep or enclosed.
- The powder cloud cannot enter the target surface.

Recommended answer:

"Rotary cup can help many groove and recess problems, but if the nozzle or powder cloud cannot physically reach the area, the result is uncertain. Please share groove width, depth, photos, and opening direction; sample testing is recommended."

### Pretreatment, curing, and powder-quality problems

Rotary cup is not the first solution when:

- Rework parts do not accept powder.
- Reclaimed powder causes poor charging or poor deposition.
- Orange peel, pinholes, or burn edges are mainly from film thickness, curing, contamination, or excessive electrostatic settings.

For rework powder problems:

- Clean the surface first.
- If needed, preheat the part to about 80-100 C.
- Keep electrostatic settings moderate; avoid simply increasing voltage.
- Use fresh powder when possible.

## Response Pattern

Use this structure:

- Fit: suitable / possible / high risk.
- Why: connect the answer to the workpiece geometry.
- Must confirm: photos, dimensions, line speed, powder type, film thickness, current gun setup.
- Next step: test spray or supplier consultation.
